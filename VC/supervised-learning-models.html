Supervised Learning Models

Breaking down the model families you reach for most in practice. Each section covers one family with the intuition behind it, and links to a standalone deep dive on each specific model.

What is supervised learning?

In supervised learning, you give the model both the inputs and the correct outputs during training. It learns the mapping from one to the other. At prediction time, it applies that learned mapping to inputs it has never seen. The "supervision" is the labeled training data.

The overwhelming majority of production ML systems run on supervised learning. Fraud detection, churn prediction, price forecasting, medical diagnosis, recommendation scoring — most of these are classification or regression problems with labeled historical data. That's the domain this series lives in.

Within supervised learning, there are three types of tasks: regression (predict a continuous number — house price, demand, temperature), classification (predict a discrete class — spam vs not, churn vs retain, disease vs healthy), and ranking (order items by relevance — search results, recommendations). The model families below all handle at least one of these.

The Four Paradigms

Supervised learning models split into four broad families based on how they build the input-to-output mapping. Knowing which family a model belongs to tells you a lot about its assumptions, failure modes, and when it's the right choice.

Family 01
Linear Models
Assume the relationship between inputs and output is linear. Fast, interpretable, and surprisingly strong when that assumption holds.
Family 02
Instance-Based
No explicit model — memorize training data and predict based on nearest neighbors at inference time. Simple, but expensive at scale.
Family 03
Probabilistic
Build a statistical model of the data distribution. Naive Bayes is the classic. Principled but sensitive to distributional assumptions.
Family 04
Tree-Based Models
Use decision trees as building blocks. No assumptions about feature distributions. Handles mixed types, missing values, and non-linear relationships naturally. This is where the deep dives below live.

01 Linear Models Coming soon

Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, Ridge, Lasso, and SVMs. These models assume the output is a linear combination of input features (or a linear decision boundary, for classification). They're fast to train, highly interpretable, and the right starting point before reaching for something more complex. When your data actually has a linear structure, they win.

The catch: real-world relationships are rarely linear. A polynomial feature set or kernel trick (SVM) can extend these models, but at that point you're often better off with a tree-based model.

02 Tree-Based Models Live

This is the most practical family for tabular data in production. Tree-based models make no assumptions about feature distributions, handle missing values, work on mixed numeric and categorical data, and naturally capture non-linear interactions between features.

They split into two camps: bagging (Random Forest — parallel, averaging) and boosting (Gradient Boosting — sequential, corrective). Boosting methods dominate Kaggle competitions on tabular data and are widely used in industry for high-stakes prediction tasks.
